The difference between Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes

Diabetes Basics: type-1 and type-2 diabetes

Ninety per cent of Canadians with diabetes have Type 2; 10 per cent have Type 1. What’s the difference between the two?

  • For people with Type 1 diabetes, their body cannot make insulin. That’s the hormone that breaks down sugar from food and converts it into energy for the body.
  • For people with Type 2 diabetes, their body can produce insulin, but it is not making enough of it or it is no longer using it well.

In both diabetes types, the buildup of sugar in the blood can rise dangerously high and lead to health problems.

Here are other ways in which Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes differ:

Risk factors:

  • Type 1 diabetes runs in families. Other risk factors are not clear.
  • Type 2 diabetes runs in families, too, but there are other known risk factors, including being overweight and/or inactive, and being over 45.

Prevention:

  • Type 1 diabetes can’t be predicted or prevented.
  • Type 2 diabetes can often be prevented by eating a well-balanced diet, getting regular exercise and maintaining a healthy body weight.

Diagnosis:

  • People are usually diagnosed with Type 1 diabetes as children or teens.
  • People develop Type 2 diabetes as they get older. Although the number of young people with Type 2 diabetes is rising, it’s still uncommon. Most people diagnosed with Type 2 diabetes are over 40.

Treatment:

  • People with Type 1 diabetes must inject insulin to manage their disease.
  • People with Type 2 diabetes might never need insulin. They might need medications to help control their blood sugar levels. Often they can also manage the disease with diet and exercise.
