People with diabetes are at risk of developing diabetic retinopathy or open-angle glaucoma, and they can get cataracts at a younger age than those who don't have diabetes.

DIY Symptom Checker: Eyes

"Sixty per cent of patients with Type 2 diabetes have some kind of retinopathy during the first 20 years they have diabetes," says Dr. Keith Gordon, vice-president research and service quality at CNIB. He notes that both open-angle glaucoma and diabetic retinopathy are often symptomless, so it's important that your loved one has their eyes tested regularly, even if their sight seems normal. Any changes to their vision could indicate the disease has advanced. Take our symptom-checker quiz.

Diabetic Retinopathy

  • Do they see spots, dots or blobs in their field of vision?
  • Do they have double vision?
  • Is their vision blurry?
  • Have they experienced vision loss?

Open-Angle Glaucoma

  • Do they have difficulty seeing in their peripheral field?

Cataracts

  • Do they see halos?
  • Do they have double vision?

When to see a doctor:

If they answer "yes" to any of these questions, ensure they get their eyes checked. "I encourage people to get regular eye exams once a year--or more often, if possible--especially if they have diabetes," says Dr. Gordon. If they experience any changes to their vision, arrange for them see an eye doctor immediately. Dr. Gordon adds that there are treatments and drugs available to help control the diseases, so he advises asking an ophthalmologist about them if your loved one has eye disease.
