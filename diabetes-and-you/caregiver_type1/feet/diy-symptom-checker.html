Feet are usually the first parts of the body to be affected by diabetes.

DIY Feet symptom checker

It starts with a loss of sensation due to damaged nerves, says Dr. Jan Hux, Chief Scientific Advisor at the Canadian Diabetes Association. “Those messages of pain that we get from our feet are really important to alert us to a little cut or a little blister, and the problem is that in people with diabetes, there’s an increased risk for infection, a decreased ability to fight infection and a decreased ability to heal.” Take our symptom-checker quiz.

  • Has your loved one lost sensation in their feet?
  • Do they feel an electric or burning sensation in their feet?
  • Do they have dry, cracked skin?
  • Do they have any sores, cuts, blisters or hangnails?
  • Do they have calluses or corns?
  • Do you see any areas of redness?
  • Are their feet swollen?
  • Have their feet or toes changed shape?
  • Are their nails thick and/or ingrown?

When to see a doctor:

If you answer “yes” to any of these questions, seek medical attention. In addition to proper-fitting shoes and socks, it’s important to schedule regular doctor’s appointments for your loved one. As a caregiver, it’s your role to help inspect their feet daily for any changes. (For example, Dr. Hux says to look where a shoe might rub and where calluses may form.) Also, talk to them about visiting a chiropodist or a podiatrist to have their nails and feet cared for professionally.
