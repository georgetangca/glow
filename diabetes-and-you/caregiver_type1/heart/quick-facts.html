Cardiovascular disease affects the circulatory system, including the heart and blood vessels.

Quick Facts: HEART

Heart failure and stroke are both types of cardiovascular disease. Diabetes is a risk factor for heart disease.

Did you know?

  • Heart disease and stroke can occur at a younger age in people with diabetes, compared to the rest of the population.
  • The risk of a cardiovascular problem increases with age, the length of time a person has had diabetes and other risk factors such as a family history of cardiovascular disease.
  • A person is less likely to survive a heart attack if they have diabetes.

How does diabetes put heart and blood vessel function at risk?

When someone has diabetes, the high blood sugar levels can eventually lead to plaque buildup on the walls of blood vessels (hardening of the arteries). And that can also cause:

  • Plaque buildup on the walls of blood vessels (hardening of the arteries)
  • High blood pressure
  • Damage to tiny blood vessels
  • Damage to nerves that help with blood flow

Reduce your risk!

For people with diabetes, the risk of a cardiovascular problem is reduced if:

  • They maintain a healthy weight
  • They eat a healthy, well-balanced diet
  • They get regular exercise
  • They don’t smoke or stop smoking
  • Their blood pressure is well controlled
  • Their blood sugar is well controlled
  • They keep their cholesterol within a healthy range

Good to know

Recognize the symptoms of a heart attack and stroke so you can get emergency help quickly and prevent severe damage to the heart or brain.

  • Heart attack: discomfort in the chest, jaw, arms and/or back; shortness of breath; sweating; nausea; and/or light-headedness.
  • Stroke: trouble speaking and seeing, headache, dizziness, and weakness.
