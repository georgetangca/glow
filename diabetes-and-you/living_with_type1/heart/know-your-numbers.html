Diabetes is linked to cardiovascular disease, so it’s important to stay on top of blood pressure.

Know your numbers: blood pressure, blood glucose and ketone levels

A blood pressure reading shows how hard the heart is pumping by measuring the force of the blood pressing on the walls of the arteries. The higher the numbers, the harder your heart is working.

Below 140/90

This is a “normal” blood pressure reading (measured in ‘mmHG’ or millimetres of mercury). The top number (systolic) measures pressure of the arteries when the heart contracts and the bottom number (diastolic) measures it when the heart relaxes.

For most people with diabetes, blood pressure of 130/80 mmHg or greater is considered high, and their doctor should monitor it.

Tracking blood sugars

A1C testing

While glucose meters assess blood sugar for the exact moment at which it’s tested, only an A1C test can take into account a much longer time frame—the past three months—to assess blood sugar control. Your physician may test for it once every three months, or even annually, if they feel it is appropriate for you.

The test is similar to blood sugar testing, in that it requires a small blood sample. But instead of strips, a cartridge is used, because a few more steps are needed to examine the sample. It takes about five minutes for results, versus the few seconds it takes for the blood sugar testing you would do every day.

You can have the A1C test done at select Shoppers Drug Mart pharmacies, and you can also buy a kit to take home. To help understand the results and to ensure proper testing procedures, it’s best to have it done at the pharmacy or have your pharmacist review the analysis.

How to check your ketone levels

What are ketones?

Ketones are created in the liver when fat is converted into energy. Glucose is energy from carbohydrates. When you don't have enough glucose to use for energy, your body will use ketones.

How are ketones tested?

To check ketone levels, a urine or blood test is given. A positive test means that there are ketones in the sample, which could be a sign of diabetic ketoacidosis. If this happens, you should contact your health care provider right away, as diabetic ketoacidosis may an early sign of kidney failure, heart attack or fluid buildup in the brain.
