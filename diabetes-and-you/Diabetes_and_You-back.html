Like any high-functioning machine, the human body needs fuel for energy. Our body uses insulin from our pancreas to make use of glucose (energy) from foods with starch, natural sugars and dairy, such as breads, pastas, fruit and milk.

Unfortunately, the pancreas of people with Type 1 diabetes cannot produce insulin. So, instead of being used for energy, glucose builds up—sometimes dangerously so—in the blood. However, Type 1 diabetes can be managed through insulin therapy—a lifeline for people with this type of diabetes.

What type of insulin is best?

When considering types of insulin, insulin regimens and blood glucose target ranges, one size definitely does not fit all. Determining what will work best depends on a variety of factors such as age, health and lifestyle. The doctor will prescribe the type and dosage. Options for insulin delivery include syringes, pens and pumps, all which have been designed to be as convenient to use and pain-free as possible.

In order to continue to accurately determine the correct dosage of insulin, you’ll need a blood glucose meter to monitor your blood glucose levels on a regular basis. There are a variety of blood glucose meters, which can be purchased from your local pharmacy. The FreeStyle InsuLinx, for example, is an advanced model meal-time insulin calculator that has been shown in studies to better protect people with Type 1 diabetes against low blood sugars, which also resulted in the use of less insulin.

How to manage diabetes

The Canadian Diabetes Association lists numerous steps people with Type 1 diabetes can take to maintain and enhance their health:

  • Take insulin according to your doctor-prescribed regimen.
  • Check blood glucose levels regularly to ensure they are within your target range.

What to watch for: Hypoglycemia

  • This is when the blood sugar is too low (below 4 mmol/L), which can be dangerous.
  • Blood sugar can drop when sick with a cold or flu, so check blood glucose levels about every two to four hours.
  • Symptoms include: headache, vision problems, trembling, sweating, difficulty focusing, stomach pains, nausea and/or fast heartbeat. Hypoglycemia may result in a seizure, fainting or even a coma.

Diabetes is the number one cause of blindness in adults under the age of 50 in Canada. Yet many people don’t realize diabetes is causing damage, until it’s too late.

Diabetic retinopathy

Diabetic retinopathy is a condition in which tiny blood vessels in the eye are damaged, or leak, from the effects of high blood sugar.

  • It’s the most common health complication of diabetes.
  • It affects half a million Canadians.
  • A fifth of people with diabetic retinopathy have a form that causes vision loss.
  • Nearly all people with Type 1 diabetes will have diabetic retinopathy in their first 20 years of living with the disease.
What other eye diseases are people at risk for when they have diabetes?

40 per cent higher risk of glaucoma

  • The risk rises with age and the length of time a person has had diabetes.

60 per cent higher risk of cataracts

  • People with diabetes develop cataracts earlier and faster.

Butt out!

  • Smoking and untreated high blood pressure can worsen eye problems from diabetes. For tips on quitting smoking for good visit quitsmoking.shoppersdrugmart.ca.

The case for an eye exam

  • People with diabetes should have an eye exam every year to check for damage.
  • A person may have no eye symptoms, yet early treatment can save their vision.

Which vitamins can I give my loved one to support their eye health?

“First, it’s important to understand why you should be concerned about their eye health. Diabetes is the single leading cause of blindness in Canada. It also raises the risk of developing blurred vision, cataracts, glaucoma, age-related macular degeneration and something called diabetic retinopathy, in which the small blood vessels in the retina become damaged.

As for how you can help them maintain their eye health: The most important thing is to stay on top of them about controlling their blood sugars and maintaining a well-balanced diet. Taking prescription medications and insulin is going to reduce the risk of those conditions. In addition, there is some evidence mounting that certain vitamins and minerals can help prevent some of these complications in patients with diabetes. We know vitamin C, vitamin A, vitamin E, zinc, copper, lutein and even beta-carotene can help. You can take them all individually or in a multi-vitamin created specifically for eye health. Check with your local pharmacist to see what is the best one for the person you are caring for.”

People with diabetes are at risk of developing diabetic retinopathy or open-angle glaucoma, and can get cataracts at a younger age than those who don’t have diabetes.

“Nearly all patients with Type 1 diabetes have some kind of retinopathy during the first 20 years they have diabetes,” says Dr. Keith Gordon, vice-president research and service quality, at CNIB. He notes that both open-angle glaucoma and diabetic retinopathy are often symptomless, so it’s important that your loved one has their eyes tested regularly, even if their sight appears to be normal. Any changes to their vision could indicate the disease has advanced. Take our symptom-checker quiz.

Diabetic Retinopathy

  • Do they see spots, dots or blobs in their field of vision?
  • Do they have double vision?
  • Is their vision blurry?
  • Have they experienced vision loss?

Open-Angle Glaucoma

  • Do they have difficulty seeing in their peripheral field?

Cataracts

  • Do they see halos?
  • Do they have double vision?
