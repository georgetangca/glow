Cardiovascular disease affects the circulatory system, including the heart and blood vessels.

Quick facts: Heart

Heart failure and stroke are both types of cardiovascular disease. Diabetes is a risk factor for heart disease.

Did you know?

  • Diabetes can increase the risk for heart disease. There is some evidence that the risk of heart attack in a middle-aged person with Type 2 diabetes is the same as someone who doesn’t have diabetes but has already had a heart attack.
  • Heart disease and stroke can occur at a younger age in people with diabetes, compared to the rest of the population.
  • Your risk of a cardiovascular problem increases with age, the length of time you’ve had diabetes, and other risk factors such as a family history of cardiovascular disease.
  • You are less likely to survive a heart attack if you have diabetes.

How does diabetes put heart and blood vessel function at risk?

When you have diabetes, the high blood sugar levels can eventually lead to plaque buildup on the walls of blood vessels (hardening of the arteries). And that can also cause:

  • High blood pressure
  • Damage to tiny blood vessels
  • Damage to nerves that help with blood flow

Reduce your risk!

For people with diabetes, the risk of a cardiovascular problem is reduced if they:

  • Maintain a healthy weight
  • Eat a healthy, well-balanced diet
  • Get regular exercise
  • Don’t smoke or stop smoking
  • Make sure their blood pressure is well controlled
  • Make sure their blood sugar is well controlled
  • Make sure their cholesterol is kept within a healthy range

Good to know

Recognize the symptoms of a heart attack and stroke so you can ask for emergency help quickly and prevent severe damage to the heart or brain.

  • Heart attack: discomfort in the chest, jaw, arms and/or back; shortness of breath; sweating; nausea; and/or light-headedness.
  • Stroke: trouble speaking and seeing, headache, dizziness, and weakness.
